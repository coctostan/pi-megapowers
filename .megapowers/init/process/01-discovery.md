# Phase 1: Discovery — Process Template

> **What we learned by doing this on megapowers, 2026-02-25**

---

## Purpose

Answer: **"Who is this for, what problem do they have, and what do they use today?"**

This is the foundation everything else builds on. Vision is aspirational, PRD is detailed, but Discovery is the ground truth about why this product should exist.

## The Four Sections

### 1. Who Is The Customer?
Define specifically. Not "developers" — which developers? Not "teams" — which teams? Include:
- What unites them (shared behavior or pain)
- Who they are NOT (explicit exclusions prevent scope creep later)

**What we learned:** The customer definition should be behavioral, not demographic. "The vibe coder who wants to build real software" is better than "developers aged 25-45." Behavioral definitions naturally answer "why would they care?" 

### 2. What Problem Do They Have?
Describe from the customer's perspective. Walk through the failure mode step by step — what happens, why it hurts, what the consequences are.

**What we learned:** The problem often has two layers. The surface problem ("AI writes untested code") and the deeper problem ("the human loses discipline too because the AI makes it feel fast"). Discovery should surface both. The deeper problem is usually the real product opportunity.

### 3. What Do They Use Today?
Catalog every alternative — including "nothing." For each:
- What it is
- How it works
- Where it falls short (relative to the problem you identified)

**What we learned:** 
- **Search broadly.** We found competitors we didn't know about (compound engineering plugin) that reshaped our understanding of the landscape.
- **Be honest about where competitors are ahead.** SP+ has better onboarding. CE is cross-platform. Acknowledging this in discovery prevents strategic blind spots.
- **Use a comparison table.** Seeing all alternatives side-by-side reveals the gap your product fills. Ours was: everyone chose soft enforcement. We chose hard enforcement.

### 4. Key Insight
One paragraph that crystallizes why this product needs to exist. This is NOT the vision (that's Phase 2). This is the market observation — the gap, the timing, the contrarian bet.

**What we learned:** The key insight often emerges from the competitive analysis, not from staring at your own product. Seeing that *everyone else* chose soft enforcement made it obvious that hard enforcement is our differentiator. We wouldn't have seen that clearly without mapping alternatives first.

---

## Open Questions

Discovery should end with explicit open questions that Phase 2 (Vision) and Phase 3 (PRD) need to resolve. These are strategic questions that can't be answered by looking at what exists — they require decisions about where to go.

**What we learned:** 4-6 open questions is the right number. Fewer means you're not thinking hard enough. More means you're trying to solve everything in discovery instead of deferring to later phases.

---

## How Long Should This Take?

For megapowers: ~1.5 hours (including competitive research).

The competitive research is the most time-consuming part but also the most valuable. Don't skip it. Don't just list competitors from memory — search for them. You'll find things you didn't expect.

## Gate

Discovery is done when:
- Customer is defined specifically (not generically)
- Problem is stated from the customer's perspective (not the builder's)
- At least 3 alternatives are cataloged with honest assessments
- Key insight exists (one paragraph)
- Open questions are listed for subsequent phases

## What Comes Next

Vision uses the key insight as its foundation and the open questions as its agenda. The vision should resolve (or explicitly defer) each open question from discovery.
